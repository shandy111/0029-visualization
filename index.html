Introduction

Why focus on public EV charging equity?

London’s low-carbon mobility transition depends not only on increasing the number of public EV chargers, but also on ensuring that charging infrastructure is fairly distributed across neighbourhoods. Previous research frames EV charging provision as a social equity issue, because uneven access may affect who can benefit from the transition to electric mobility (Hopkins et al., 2023). This is especially important in London, where many residents lack access to private off-street parking or home charging and may therefore rely more heavily on public or on-street charging facilities (Zhang et al., 2024; Budnitz et al., 2024).

Charging equity matters

Public EV charging is not only a transport infrastructure issue, but also an urban equity issue because uneven provision can limit who benefits from electric mobility (Hopkins et al., 2023).

Home-charging constraints matter

Residents without private driveways, garages, or off-street parking may face greater barriers to EV adoption and rely more on local public charging (Budnitz et al., 2024).

London is a relevant case

London combines ambitious low-emission transport policies with uneven neighbourhood conditions, making it a useful case for examining public EV charging accessibility (Zhang et al., 2024).

Neighbourhood scale matters

LSOA-level analysis helps reveal local charging gaps that may be hidden by broader borough-level averages or city-wide charger totals (Zhang et al., 2024).

Deprivation and basic access

Does deprivation explain the gap?

The Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) measures socioeconomic disadvantage across England. We examine whether more deprived areas systematically have fewer public EV chargers, and find that while a relationship exists, it is not sufficient on its own to explain London's charging geography. Car ownership patterns provide additional demand-side context rather than causal proof.

Borough-level differences

Borough-level differences and uneven rollout patterns

Borough-level variation remains one of the clearest patterns in the London EV charging landscape. Some boroughs such as Westminster and Hammersmith & Fulham shows a much higher public charger density than several boroughs in east and outer London. This pattern is consistent with uneven local rollout, but it does not by itself prove a single policy explanation.

Discussion

Key Findings

Our analysis suggests that EV charging inequality in London reflects a combination of geography, uneven rollout patterns, deprivation and demand-side context.

Spatial divide

Inner London boroughs have significantly higher charger density, reflecting both stronger existing provision and a clear inner–outer spatial divide.

Borough differences matter

The gap between highest (Hammersmith & Fulham, 94.5/10k) and lowest (Havering, 1.4/10k) boroughs is 69×, far exceeding what deprivation alone can explain.

Deprivation is a secondary factor

While IMD correlates with charger access, its explanatory power is limited and does not fully explain the observed charging pattern across London.

Demand-side context

Housing type, occupation and transport accessibility provide important context, but do not fully account for the observed inequality in public charging provision.
