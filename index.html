INTRODUCTION

Why does EV charging inequality matter?

The UK government has committed to phasing out new petrol and diesel cars by 2035. But the success of this transition depends critically on equitable access to charging infrastructure. If public chargers are concentrated in wealthier or more central areas, the green transition risks deepening existing urban inequalities.

This project investigates three key questions: Where are London's public chargers located? What factors explain their uneven distribution? And does borough-level policy play a stronger role than socioeconomic deprivation?

STEP A

Spatial inequality exists

Charger density varies dramatically across London's 4,994 LSOAs, with a clear inner–outer divide.

STEP B

Deprivation alone doesn't explain it

IMD and demand-side factors partially correlate with access, but leave much of the variation unexplained.

STEP C

Borough policy is the stronger driver

After controlling for demand-side factors, borough-level differences emerge as the key explanatory variable.

STEP B · PART 1

Does deprivation explain the gap?

The Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) measures socioeconomic disadvantage across England. We examine whether more deprived areas systematically have fewer public EV chargers — and find that while a relationship exists, it is weaker than expected. Car ownership patterns further contextualise demand-side variation across neighbourhoods.

STEP C — BOROUGH POLICY

Borough policy as the key driver

After accounting for demand-side factors, borough-level variation emerges as the strongest predictor of charger density. Boroughs with proactive EV infrastructure policies — such as Westminster and Hammersmith & Fulham — show dramatically higher provision than comparable areas in east and outer London.

DISCUSSION

Key Findings

Our analysis reveals that EV charging inequality in London is primarily driven by geography and local policy, rather than socioeconomic deprivation alone.

Spatial divide

Inner London boroughs have significantly higher charger density, reflecting both higher demand and stronger policy commitment from local authorities.

Borough policy matters

The gap between highest (Hammersmith & Fulham, 94.5/10k) and lowest (Havering, 1.4/10k) boroughs is 69×, far exceeding what deprivation alone can explain.

Deprivation is a secondary factor

While IMD correlates with charger access, its explanatory power is limited once spatial and policy factors are controlled for.

Demand-side context

Housing type, occupation and transport accessibility provide important context, but do not fully account for the observed inequality in public charging provision.
